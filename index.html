What is menstruation?

The menstrual cycle is a natural, monthly process that occurs in people with female reproductive systems, typically starting during adolescence and continuing until menopause. It involves a series of hormonal and physiological changes in the body that prepare it for the possibility of pregnancy. The key stages of the menstrual cycle are: menstruation, follicular phase, ovulation, luteal phase. If fertilization does occur, the fertilized egg (zygote) will implant into the uterus, and the pregnancy process begins. If not, the uterine lining is shed once again, marking the start of a new menstrual cycle.

The length of the menstrual cycle can vary from person to person but is typically around 28 days. However, cycles can be shorter or longer (ranging from 21 to 35 days) and still be considered normal. Irregularities in the menstrual cycle can be influenced by various factors, including stress, illness, hormonal imbalances, and more. Tracking your menstrual cycle can be helpful for understanding your reproductive health and can be used for family planning or identifying potential issues.

menses phase

The menses phase starts off the cycle from days 1-5 with menstruation, also known as a period. During this phase, the lining of the uterus (endometrium) sheds and is expelled through the vagina. This typically lasts for 3 to 7 days.

If pregnancy does not occur, estrogen and progesterone levels drop, which can contribute to mood swings and heightened emotional sensitivity in some women. This phase may also be associated with physical discomfort, which can further influence emotions.

follicular phase

After menstruation, the body starts preparing for a potential pregnancy from days 1-13. The brain's pituitary gland releases follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which stimulates the ovaries to produce several follicles, each containing an immature egg (oocyte). These follicles start to mature, but usually, only one will reach full maturity.

During the first half of the menstrual cycle, estrogen levels gradually rise. This increase in estrogen can have mood-lifting effects, promoting feelings of well-being and positivity in many individuals.

ovulation phase

Around the middle of the menstrual cycle (day 14), a surge in luteinizing hormone (LH) triggers the release of the matured egg from one of the ovarian follicles. This is known as ovulation. The egg then travels through the fallopian tube, where it can potentially be fertilized by sperm.

Around the time of ovulation, estrogen levels peak, and some women may experience increased energy, self-confidence, and heightened mood. This is often referred to as the "ovulatory peak."

luteal phase

After ovulation (day 15-28), the empty follicle transforms into a structure called the corpus luteum. The corpus luteum secretes progesterone and some estrogen, which help prepare the uterine lining for a possible embryo implantation. If fertilization doesn't occur, the corpus luteum breaks down, leading to a drop in progesterone and estrogen levels, and this triggers the start of a new menstrual cycle.

In the second half of the menstrual cycle, both estrogen and progesterone levels rise. Progesterone can have a sedative effect, leading to feelings of relaxation but, for some individuals, may also contribute to mood changes such as irritability, anxiety, or sadness.

effects of menstruation on women

Women often experience varying emotional and physical responses during each menstrual cycle.

Women experience emotional responses during their menstrual cycle due to the fluctuation of hormones, primarily estrogen and progesterone, which play a significant role in regulating mood and emotions. These hormonal changes can influence neurotransmitters in the brain, such as serotonin and dopamine. However, factors such as genetics, stress levels, lifestyle, and underlying mental health conditions can also play a role in how hormones affect emotions during menstruation. Some women may experience premenstrual syndrome (PMS) or more severe premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD), which can lead to more pronounced emotional symptoms.

types of period products

  • Disposable pads:

    • These are made of absorbent materials and have an adhesive backing to stick to underwear. They come in various sizes and thicknesses.

  • Disposable tampons:

    • These are cylindrical, absorbent products inserted into the vaginal canal to absorb menstrual flow. They come with or without applicators.

  • Menstrual cups:

    • These are reusable, bell-shaped cups made of silicone, rubber, or other materials. They are inserted into the vagina to collect menstrual blood and can be emptied and reused.
